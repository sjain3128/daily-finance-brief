EXPLAINED🟢 EDUCATIONALMacro
📚 Why Rising US Treasury Yields Hurt Your Indian Stock Portfolio
When US Treasury yields rise (currently at 19-year highs of 5.19%), global investors reallocate from riskier assets — like Indian stocks and EM bonds — to safer US government bonds offering compelling returns. This is the mechanics behind India's $21.5B FPI exodus in 2026. Additionally, higher US yields typically mean a stronger dollar, which weakens the rupee and makes India's oil import bill even more expensive (a double whammy). The "yield spread" — the extra return India must offer vs US Treasuries to attract investors — narrows at high US yields, reducing India's relative appeal. Every basis point rise in the US 10-year affects global capital flows into India.

IEA / GEOGRAPHY🟡 HIGH RELEVANCECommodities
📚 The Strait of Hormuz: Why One 33km Waterway Controls Global Fuel Prices
The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow passage between Iran and Oman — only 33 km wide at its narrowest. Yet ~20–21 million barrels of oil per day pass through it, representing about 20% of global oil supply and 30% of global LNG. Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, and Qatar all depend on it for their oil exports. Iran's unique ability to threaten (or block) this chokepoint is what gives it outsized geopolitical leverage and why the US-Iran war has had such an extreme market impact. India sources a significant portion of its Gulf oil imports through routes exposed to Hormuz disruptions.

FINSHOTS STYLE🟢 EDUCATIONALMacro Concept
📚 What Is Stagflation and Why Are Economists Worried About It Right Now?
Stagflation = Stagnant/slowing growth + High inflation occurring simultaneously. It is an economist's policy nightmare because the standard cures conflict: raising rates to fight inflation also slows growth, while cutting rates to boost growth worsens inflation. Central banks have no clean solution. The 1970s oil shocks created the last major global stagflation episode. Today's combination of a 55%+ surge in crude oil prices, disrupted global supply chains, and a weakening growth outlook is recreating similar conditions. For investors: stagflation tends to be bad for bonds (inflation erodes real returns) and growth stocks (high rates compress valuations), but historically positive for gold, real assets, and commodity producers.
